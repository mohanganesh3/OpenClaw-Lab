## Problem
Current machine learning potentials struggle with uncertainty quantification, limiting their reliability for active learning, calibration, and out-of-distribution detection.

## Core Claim
Bayesian E(3)-equivariant MLPs with joint energy-force uncertainty modeling achieve competitive accuracy while enabling robust uncertainty quantification.

## Key Contributions
- Development of Bayesian E(3)-equivariant MLPs with iterative restratification of many-body message passing
- Introduction of joint energy-force negative log-likelihood (NLLJEF) loss function
- Systematic benchmarking of multiple Bayesian approaches (deep ensembles, SWAG, IVON, MVE)

## Method
The approach develops Bayesian E(3)-equivariant MLPs using deep ensembles with joint energy-force uncertainty modeling through the NLLJEF loss function, combined with iterative restratification of many-body message passing.
- Components: Bayesian neural network architecture with E(3)-equivariance, Joint energy-force uncertainty modeling via NLLJEF loss, Iterative restratification of many-body message passing, Multiple Bayesian approaches (DE, SWAG, IVON, MVE)
- Key innovation: The joint energy-force negative log-likelihood (NLLJEF) loss function, which explicitly models uncertainties in both energies and forces simultaneously, reducing energy errors by 3-4 times and force errors by 2-3 times compared to energy-only uncertainty modeling.
- Assumptions: Joint modeling of energy and force uncertainties leads to better overall performance; E(3)-equivariance is beneficial for interatomic potentials; The datasets used (oBN25, 3BPA) are representative for evaluation

## Critical Reading
- Stated limitations: The method is specifically designed for interatomic potentials in molecular systems; Computational requirements may be high for large-scale applications
- Unstated weaknesses: Limited applicability to domains outside of molecular physics; May not address specific challenges in medical image classification
- Threats to validity: Domain mismatch between molecular physics and medical imaging; Assumptions about physical equivariance may not translate to medical images
