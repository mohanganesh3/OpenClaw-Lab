# EV051: Out of Distribution Detection via Neural Network Anchoring

## Problem
Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, which involves distinguishing between in-distribution samples from training data and out-of-distribution samples with different semantic characteristics.

## Core Claim
The proposed anchoring training strategy enables heteroscedastic temperature scaling (different temperature parameters per sample) to achieve state-of-the-art OOD detection performance without requiring exposure to additional outlier data, custom calibration objectives, or model ensembling.

## Key Contributions
- Proposed a new training strategy called anchoring that can estimate appropriate temperature values for each sample
- Demonstrated through NTK theory that the temperature function estimate is closely linked to the epistemic uncertainty of the classifier
- Achieved state-of-the-art OOD detection performance across several benchmarks without requiring exposure to additional outlier data, custom calibration objectives, or model ensembling

## Method
The method uses neural network anchoring during training to transform inputs into [c, x-c] tuples, estimates temperature values for each sample based on prediction variance across multiple anchors during inference, applies heteroscedastic calibration Hc(y|x) = H(y|x)τ(x), and computes the Anchor Marginalized Prediction (AMP) score for OOD detection.
- Components: Neural network anchoring: Transforming inputs into [c, x-c] tuples, Temperature estimation: Computing τ(x) as a function of prediction variance across anchors, Heteroscedastic calibration: Scaling logits by the estimated temperature, AMP scoring: Using negative log likelihood of calibrated predictions as the OOD score
- Key innovation: The anchoring mechanism that creates an ensembling-like behavior by producing different predictions conditioned on the anchor choice, allowing estimation of epistemic uncertainty through prediction variance across anchors.
- Assumptions: The neural network width tends to infinity and learning rate tends to zero (NTK theory assumptions); Anchors are randomly chosen during training and inference; For in-distribution samples, predictions across different anchors should converge to the same value

## Experimental Setup
- Datasets: CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, ImageNet-C, SCOOD (Texture, SVHN, Tiny-ImageNet, LSUN, Places365), LSUN resizing variants
- Baselines: Mahalanobis detector, Gram Matrices, ResNet-50 Temp-Scal, Deep Ens, MCD, SVI, SVI-AvUC, ODIN, Energy, OE, UDG, MSP, GM
- Metrics: AUROC, FPR95, AUPR-in/out, DTACC
- Compute: Not specified

## Results
Headline: AMP achieves 99.07 AUROC on ImageNet-C with ResNet-50

| Method | Dataset | Metric | Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| AMP | ImageNet-C | AUROC | 99.07 |
| AMP | ImageNet-C | DTACC | 95.14 |
| AMP | ImageNet-C | AUPR-in/out | 99.10/99.08 |
| AMP | SCOOD (CIFAR-10) | FPR95 | 36.82 |
| AMP | SCOOD (CIFAR-10) | AUROC | 92.40 |
| AMP | SCOOD (CIFAR-10) | AUPR-in/out | 91.23/90.91 |
| AMP | SCOOD (CIFAR-100) | FPR95 | 70.34 |
| AMP | SCOOD (CIFAR-100) | AUROC | 82.22 |

- Ablations: Transformations (ColorJitter/GaussianBlur) reduce FPR95 from 50.71 to 6.53; ResizedCrop reduces FPR95 to 5.32; Increasing anchors from 2 to 20 has minimal impact (FPR95 variation <1%)
- Improvement over baseline: AMP outperforms baselines by 2.69 AUROC points on ImageNet-C (99.07 vs 96.38), 3.62 AUROC points on SCOOD CIFAR-10 (92.40 vs 88.78), and achieves comparable FPR95 to best baseline (36.82 vs 36.22) without outlier exposure

## Critical Reading
- Stated limitations: The method might be computationally intensive since it estimates temperature for each sample; The theoretical connection to NTK theory might be more conceptual than practical
- Unstated weaknesses: The method might not scale well to very large medical imaging datasets; The anchoring mechanism might be sensitive to hyperparameters; The benchmarks might not fully represent real-world medical imaging scenarios
- Threats to validity: The method's performance might degrade with domain shifts not covered in benchmarks; The connection to epistemic uncertainty might be correlational rather than causal; The method might not work well with very deep architectures common in medical imaging
- Open questions: How well does the method perform with different neural architectures?; Can the anchoring mechanism be applied to other calibration techniques?; How does the method perform with limited training data common in medical imaging?
