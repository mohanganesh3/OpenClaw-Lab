## Problem
Calibration of machine learning models is brittle and easily lost under minimal covariate shifts between training and testing distributions.

## Core Claim
An importance sampling based approach can effectively calibrate models under covariate shift without requiring labeled target data.

## Key Contributions
- Formal introduction of the problem of calibration under domain shift
- Proposal of an importance sampling based approach to address calibration under covariate shift
- Evaluation of the method on both real-world and synthetic datasets

## Method
The method weights the calibration loss on training data by the density ratio γ(x) = πte(x)/πtr(x), enabling calibration under covariate shift without labeled target data.
- Components: Covariate shift assumption (πtr(X) ≠ πte(X) but πtr(Y|X) = πte(Y|X)), Importance sampling based weighting of calibration loss, Application to existing calibration techniques (like isotonic regression)
- Key innovation: Theorem 4.1 proving that calibration loss under covariate shift is equivalent to density ratio weighted calibration loss on training data.
- Assumptions: Access to labeled training data from source distribution; Access to unlabeled test data from target distribution; Covariate shift assumption: πtr(X) ≠ πte(X) but πtr(Y|X) = πte(Y|X); Support of test distribution includes support of training distribution (supp(πte) ⊃ supp(πtr))

## Critical Reading
- Stated limitations: Existing techniques, including domain adaptation ones, primarily focus on prediction accuracy and do not guarantee calibration; Calibration is brittle and can be easily lost under minimal covariate shifts
- Unstated weaknesses: No discussion of computational complexity or scalability for high-dimensional medical images; No analysis of sensitivity to proposal distribution choice in importance sampling; No consideration of class imbalance common in medical datasets; No evaluation of different types of covariate shifts; No ablation studies to isolate component contributions
- Threats to validity: Synthetic datasets may not adequately represent real-world medical imaging shifts; Limited generalizability assessment beyond evaluated domains; No hyperparameter sensitivity analysis; No long-term calibration stability evaluation
- Open questions: How does method perform with feature vs. prior probability shifts?; Can importance sampling be optimized for high-dimensional medical images?; What is sensitivity to proposal distribution selection?; Does calibration maintain over continuous distribution shifts?
