## Problem
The difficulty of obtaining reliable labeled data for supervised machine learning in medical imaging, necessitating semi-supervised or unsupervised approaches like anomaly detection.

## Core Claim
Anomaly detection methods effectively address medical imaging tasks like classification and segmentation by leveraging semi-supervised and unsupervised learning, reducing dependence on costly labeled data.

## Key Contributions
- Semi-exhaustive literature review of 120 papers on anomaly detection in medical imaging, clustered into applications (ocular, chest X-ray/mammography, CT/fMRI, brain MRI, mixed domains).
- Identification of dominant research trends: brain MRI applications (53.85% of papers), prevalence of deviation-based methods using autoencoders/GANs (69.23%), and dataset-driven research bias.
- Demonstration of successful applications in tumor detection (7/15 brain MRI papers), pneumonia/COVID-19 detection in chest X-rays, and retina lesion segmentation in OCT.

## Method
Qualitative literature review using Google Scholar and 4 search terms, filtering 120 papers to 45 relevant research items, then clustering by imaging modality and analyzing applications, methods, and motivations.
- Components: Semi-exhaustive literature search and filtering, Categorization of papers by imaging modality (Tables I-V), Analysis of applied methods (e.g., AEs, GANs, CNNs), Identification of research motivations (e.g., labeled data scarcity)
- Key innovation: The systematic clustering of anomaly detection applications across medical imaging domains, revealing the dominance of brain MRI research and deviation-based methods, and linking research trends to dataset availability.
- Assumptions: Anomalies in medical images can be defined as deviations from normal/healthy samples.; Publicly available datasets drive research focus in specific imaging domains.; Semi-supervised/unsupervised methods can effectively replace supervised approaches where labels are scarce.
