## Problem
Fairness audits require large labeled datasets which are costly and labor-intensive to obtain in biomedical applications.

## Core Claim
Infairness framework enables reliable fairness auditing with limited labeled data by combining small labeled datasets with large unlabeled datasets through semi-supervised inference.

## Key Contributions
- Introduction of Infairness unified framework for auditing multiple fairness criteria using semi-supervised inference
- Demonstration of robustness to ML and imputation model specifications
- Empirical validation showing 50% variance reduction compared to supervised estimation in real-world audits

## Method
Combines small labeled dataset with large unlabeled dataset by imputing missing outcomes via regression with nonlinear basis functions, then evaluates fairness criteria on the completed dataset.
- Components: Labeled data processing for initial parameter estimation, Unlabeled data imputation using regression with nonlinear basis functions, Fairness criterion evaluation on imputed dataset
- Key innovation: Leveraging auxiliary covariates (W) through carefully specified imputation models to improve efficiency while maintaining robustness to model misspecification.
- Assumptions: Availability of auxiliary covariates W for imputation; Appropriate selection of nonlinear basis functions for imputation model; Fairness criteria can be expressed as functions of model predictions and ground truth outcomes

## Experimental Setup
- Datasets: MIMIC-III
- Baselines: Supervised (SUP), Beta Calibration (BC), Nonparametric (NP), Infairness
- Metrics: TPR (True Positive Rate), ∆TPR (difference in TPR)
- Compute: null

## Results
Headline: Infairness achieves lowest bias and variance for ∆TPR estimation on MIMIC-III

| Method | Dataset | Metric | Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Infairness | MIMIC-III | ∆TPR bias | 0.02 |
| Infairness | MIMIC-III | ∆TPR variance | 0.05 |
| BC | MIMIC-III | ∆TPR bias | 0.08 |
| NP | MIMIC-III | ∆TPR bias | 0.10 |
| SUP | MIMIC-III | ∆TPR bias | 0.15 |

- Ablations: Infairness(S) (nonlinear S only); Infairness(S+W) (nonlinear S + additive W); Infairness(S×W) (nonlinear S + additive W + interactions)
- Improvement over baseline: Infairness reduces ∆TPR bias by 87.5% compared to SUP (0.02 vs 0.15)

## Critical Reading
- Stated limitations: Limited to fairness auditing applications rather than general semi-supervised learning; May not address all types of fairness criteria comprehensively; Assumes availability of unlabeled data which might not always be accessible
- Unstated weaknesses: Does not directly address label noise, which is central to the research project; May not be suitable for all types of medical image data; The imputation method might struggle with highly imbalanced subpopulations; Theoretical analysis might not cover all practical scenarios
- Threats to validity: Generalization risk: The approach may not generalize well to different medical domains or imaging modalities; Confounding factors: The imputation process might introduce biases if not carefully designed; Sample selection bias: The labeled data might not be representative of the overall population; Model misspecification: The robustness claims might not hold for all types of ML models
- Open questions: How does the approach perform under different levels and types of label noise?; Can the method be adapted for image-based fairness auditing specifically?; How does the imputation quality affect the fairness audit results?; What are the computational requirements for large-scale medical image datasets?
