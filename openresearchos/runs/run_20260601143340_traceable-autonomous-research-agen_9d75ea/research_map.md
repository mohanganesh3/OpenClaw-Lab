## Gap Map

### G01

Current autonomous research agents lack comprehensive traceability mechanisms for their decision-making processes and research actions. While papers like 'The AI Scientist' demonstrate automated discovery capabilities, they don't provide frameworks for maintaining detailed logs of agent reasoning, hypothesis generation, and experimental choices throughout the research lifecycle.

Evidence: EV001, EV002, EV003

Keywords: traceability, autonomous agents, decision logging, research provenance

### G02

Existing evaluation benchmarks like MLE-bench and AgentBench focus on task completion metrics but don't adequately assess the quality and completeness of traceability in autonomous research agents. There's no standardized way to evaluate whether an agent's traceable outputs are sufficient for scientific reproducibility and verification.

Evidence: EV004, EV009

Keywords: evaluation, traceability, benchmarks, research agents

### G03

While Reflexion and Self-Refine papers show promise for agent improvement through iterative refinement, there's no clear framework for maintaining traceability when agents iteratively refine their approaches. The relationship between self-improvement mechanisms and comprehensive action logging remains unexplored.

Evidence: EV006, EV007

Keywords: reflexion, self-refinement, traceability, iterative improvement

### G04

Embodied agents like Voyager demonstrate physical interaction capabilities, but there's limited research on maintaining traceability when agents interact with physical or virtual environments during research. The challenge of logging sensor data, environmental interactions, and resulting decisions in real-time remains unsolved.

Evidence: EV008

Keywords: embodied agents, traceability, physical interaction, research environments

## Quality Rule

These gaps are hypotheses for research planning, not claims of novelty. The Novelty Tribunal must search for near-identical prior work before an idea advances.
