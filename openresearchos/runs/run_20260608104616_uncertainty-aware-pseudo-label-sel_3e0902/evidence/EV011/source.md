# EV011: Robust Specific Emitter Identification Under Label Noise and Quantity Limitations in Intelligent Transportation Systems

## Abstract

As a crucial authentication technology at the physical layer, Specific Emitter Identification (SEI) enhances the reliability and privacy of traffic communication, thereby improving the security of Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS). Currently, deep learning-based SEI (DL-SEI) with powerful feature extraction has become a research focal point. Most DL-SEI methods rely on high-quality datasets, which require sufficient samples and accurate labelling. However, with the trend of increasing the scale of datasets, the limited availability of labeled data and the presence of labeling noise are unavoidable, leading to a reduction in their robustness. To address the above challenges, this paper presents a noise-robust SEI (NR-SEI), which effectively takes advantage of limited data while mitigating the effects of label noise. Specifically, NR-SEI employs the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) as a global cleanliness measure, combined with k-means clustering to partition input samples into clean and noisy subsets. A robust semi-supervised learning framework is then designed to leverage both clean and noisy samples. For clean samples, label optimization is performed by combining predictions from both clean and frequency-domain augmented samples, followed by temperature sharpening to reduce over-reliance on clean labels. For noisy samples, the high-confidence pseudo-label and pseudo-label smoothing strategies are used to reduce the negative impact of label noise on model training. In comparative experiments on both Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast (ADS-B) and Automatic Identification System (AIS) datasets, the proposed NR-SEI outperforms all existing approaches. NR-SEI maintains notable performance even when faced with 80% unlabeled data and asymmetric label noise. For the ADS-B dataset, NR-SEI can achieve accuracies of 95.51% and 86.44% when the noise ratios are 20% and 40%, respectively. For the AIS dataset, it achieves 94.83% and 94.06% accuracies at the corresponding noise levels.
