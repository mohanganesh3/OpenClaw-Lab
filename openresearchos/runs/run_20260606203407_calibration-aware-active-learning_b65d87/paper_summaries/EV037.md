## Problem
Standard benchmarks cannot determine how close neural networks are to their theoretical optimum because they lack access to the true posterior p(y|x).

## Core Claim
Neural networks continue to learn and improve in epistemic uncertainty even when total loss plateaus, and this learning can only be measured with access to exact posteriors.

## Key Contributions
- Using class-conditional normalizing flows as oracles to make exact posteriors tractable on realistic images
- Decomposing prediction error into irreducible aleatoric uncertainty and reducible epistemic error
- Demonstrating epistemic error follows power laws in dataset size even when total loss plateaus
- Showing architectures differ markedly in how they approach the aleatoric floor
- Demonstrating training with exact posteriors (soft labels) outperforms hard labels
- Enabling exact quantification of distribution shift via KL divergence
- Using epistemic uncertainty to distinguish informative samples for active learning

## Method
The authors use class-conditional normalizing flows as oracles to generate exact posterior distributions p(y|x) for realistic images, enabling decomposition of prediction error into aleatoric and epistemic components, and facilitating controlled experiments on distribution shift and active learning.
- Components: Class-conditional normalizing flows as oracles, Error decomposition into aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, Power law fitting for epistemic error scaling, KL divergence computation for distribution shift, Epistemic uncertainty-based sample selection for active learning
- Key innovation: The use of class-conditional normalizing flows as oracles to make exact posteriors tractable on realistic images, which enables measurement of the true posterior p(y|x) and all subsequent analyses.
- Assumptions: Class-conditional normalizing flows can accurately model the true posterior p(y|x); The oracle distributions are representative of real-world data distributions; The decomposition of error into aleatoric and epistemic components is valid

## Experimental Setup
- Datasets: AFHQ, ImageNet-64, ImageNet-128, ImageNet (1000 classes)
- Baselines: ResNets, Vision Transformers (ViTs)
- Metrics: KL divergence (epistemic error), accuracy, Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), Inception Score (IS), manifold coverage, semantic feature alignment
- Compute: 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs

## Results
Headline: Epistemic error follows power-law decay (KL ∝ N^{-α}) across architectures and scales to ImageNet-1000

- Ablations: Architectural inductive biases (convolutional vs. transformer)
- Improvement over baseline: ResNets reduce epistemic error by 6× vs. 1.2× for ViTs in low-data regimes

## Critical Reading
- Stated limitations: Uses AFHQ and ImageNet datasets rather than medical images; Computational requirements for normalizing flows may be high; Focuses on classification tasks without explicitly addressing active learning
- Unstated weaknesses: Doesn't address domain-specific challenges in medical imaging; Scalability to high-resolution medical images is unclear; Limited connection to active learning strategies; Computational feasibility for medical applications not addressed
- Threats to validity: Generalizability from natural images to medical images is uncertain; Assumption of 'exact' posteriors from normalizing flows may not hold for medical images; Architectural comparisons may not extend to specialized medical imaging architectures
- Open questions: How do these findings translate to medical image classification tasks?; Can the exact posterior approach be made computationally feasible for medical imaging?; How can the scaling laws inform active learning strategies for medical images?; What are the aleatoric floors for common medical imaging tasks?
